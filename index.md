## [Yo soy un teléfono: Playful procedural poetry with Tracery and P5.js](tracetry.html)
Playful pedagogy enjoys a long history in second language acquisition (SLA) classrooms, with scholars looking at humor as a way to embrace the inevitable mistakes learners make. Language instructors commonly use nursery rhymes, and games like telephone and charades. When I taught middle school English Language Arts, I used poems like Lewis Carrol’s “Jabberwoky” in grammar instruction and silly nonsense poems to teach grammatical structure.

This work is inspired by listening to my children (9 and 11 years old at the time) piece together Spanish words they learned (through Duolingo) into intentionally incorrect sentences to amuse one another. Notably, in the middle of dinner one evening, my 11-year-old spoke in a slow, robotic tone, “Yo. Soy. Un. Teléfono.” I looked at her and translated, “You are a telephone?” and she and her brother burst into gleeful laughter.

My children’s language games inspired me to create a digital version of this play using Tracery to generate poetry (Tracetry?) as a playful way to interact with Spanish vocabulary and grammar. This creative project was intended as an experiment with the ways that genAI tools can assist someone like me (with only basic computational thinking skills and experience with Tracery) in creating a more complicated project in OpenProcessing.

I began with a copy of a [sketch created by Anastasia Salter for a class demonstration](https://openprocessing.org/sketch/1325975) and then worked with ChatGPT-4o and Claude 3.7 to embed my Tracery grammar into the OpenProcessing P5.js code. The sentence structures are simple (to stay within my own basic knowledge of Spanish; I also used Google Translate to check several translations and spellings). The final version includes six sentence configurations along the lines of, "#noun# #adjective# #verb# #adverb#."

Other configurations include simple sentence beginnings like “Yo soy” (I am), and all nouns and adjectives are in the masculine form to maintain a simple grammatical structure. Though grammatical, sentences generated are as silly as my 11-year-old’s example, such as, “¿Dónde está el televisor guapo?” (Where is the handsome television?).
